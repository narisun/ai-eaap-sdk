Two teams.
One platform.
Zero drift.

A shared SDK lets the platform team own the hard parts once, so the business AI team can focus on the parts that move the business.

This document makes the case for adopting ai-core-sdk as the shared substrate for every agent, MCP tool, and RAG service we build.

It is written for two audiences whose work meets at the SDK boundary: the Platform / Tech AI Team who will maintain it, and the Business AI Team who will build on top of it.

§ 01The premise

AI agents are easy to demo.
The hard part is operating them.

A working prototype is one prompt and a few lines of LangGraph. A working fleet is the moment every cross-cutting concern shows up at once: authentication, authorisation, audit, observability, retries, timeouts, schema versioning, secret resolution, MCP connections, token budgets, PII redaction, and a test seam that doesn't require a live database.

We can solve those concerns once, in a shared SDK, and audit them carefully — or we can solve them eight times, in eight different teams, with eight different bugs. This document argues for once.

The SDK is not a framework you have to use. It is the agreement between the team who keeps production safe and the team who builds the things production runs.
§ 02Two audiences, one contract

Two teams meet at the SDK boundary.

Adopting the SDK requires a clear split of responsibility. We define two groups, and the SDK is the contract between them.

Group A · maintains the SDK

Platform / Tech AI Team

ArchitectsSoftware EngineersDevOpsProduction Support

Owns the runtime. Implements the cross-cutting concerns once, with strong tests, clear contracts, and structured error semantics. Provides safe defaults so a careless agent can't ship an unsafe configuration. On call when something at the platform layer breaks.

Group B · builds on the SDK

Business / Functional AI Team

Agent EngineersMCP Tool AuthorsRAG EngineersDomain Experts

Owns the agents. Translates business problems into prompts, tools, schemas, and evaluations. Picks models, tunes them, measures them against business outcomes. Treats the SDK as a stable substrate rather than something to fork or work around.

§ 03For the platform team

Cross-cutting concerns the SDK owns once, for everyone.

Each of these is a non-negotiable for production AI in a regulated environment. The SDK provides them all behind well-defined seams so individual agent code stays small and focused.

  1. Authentication & Authorisation Pluggable JWT verification plus an OPA policy evaluator at every tool and HTTP boundary. fail_closed by default. NoOpPolicyEvaluator for local dev only.
  2. Audit pipeline Structured AuditRecord events fan out to file, Sentry, Datadog. Redactor chain runs before any sink. Buffered writes via a single sink contract that never raises.
  3. Observability OpenTelemetry traces + LangFuse generations + structlog with task-isolated baggage. One trace_id correlates spans across agents, tools, and audit events.
  4. Budget enforcement BudgetService with per-tenant and per-(tenant, agent) overrides. Daily reset. Token + USD limits. Latency SLO checks.
  5. Retry & timeout Tenacity-backed policies on every LLM call with consistent jitter, status-aware retry, and timeout-aware cancellation. No team writes their own.
  6. Schema versioning Versioned Pydantic schemas at every tool boundary. Inputs validated on ingress, outputs validated on egress, with a @validate_tool decorator that wraps sync or async handlers.
  7. Secret management ISecretManager abstraction over env, AWS, Vault. Secrets are opaque references; never inlined; never logged. Backends bound by the DI module.
  8. MCP integration Connection pool with idle TTL, health checks, and lifecycle. Late-binding-safe tool resolution. One integration, audited once.
  9. Memory & compaction Token-aware summarisation with a configurable budget. Compaction wrapped in asyncio.wait_for so a slow LLM cannot hang an agent.
  10. DI & test seam A real DI container is the only seam. Fakes provided for every interface — FakePolicyEvaluator, FakeBudgetService, FakeObservabilityProvider. No monkey-patching, no real DB in unit tests.

Each concern is the product of weeks of design and review. Rebuilding any one of them in a single agent team would absorb a quarter's worth of senior engineering time. Rebuilding all of them, in every team, is roughly how organisations end up with the failure modes in the next section.

§ 04For the platform team

What happens without a shared SDK.

Each of these scenarios has a counterpart we have personally watched in production. They are not hypothetical risk theatre. They are the recurring cost of letting every team solve cross-cutting concerns in isolation.

02:14 — Tuesday

The 2 a.m. incident

Billing-agent latency p99 spikes to 8 s. You have logs in three formats from three teams, no shared trace_id, and no way to follow a request across agents. Mean time to root cause: hours.

One trace_id per request, propagated via OTel baggage and structlog ContextVar. Logs, traces, and audit events all join on it. Mean time to root cause: minutes.

$34,000 — overnight

The midnight budget burn

A tool retries on every 500 from a flaky vendor. By morning the agent has burned through tens of thousands of dollars of inference credits and your CFO has questions.

BudgetService rejects calls past the daily limit. The agent returns a graceful error, the alert fires at the configured threshold, and you find out at 09:00 — without the bill.

Q3 audit window

The compliance audit

A regulator asks for a complete trail of AI-driven decisions affecting customer data. Each team scrambles to reconstruct logs from three or four sources. Nothing has the same shape. Some events were never written.

A single AuditRecord stream with a stable schema, redactor chain, and fan-out to your SIEM. Query by tool_name, tenant_id, decision_path. Complete, since day one.

Friday afternoon

The leaked PII

A new engineer prints a customer email in a debug log to track down a bug. It ships to production logs verbatim and gets indexed by your log aggregator. Now you have a notification obligation.

Audit goes through BufferedAuditSink; a redactor chain strips PII before any sink writes. Even careless code can't leak through the audit pipeline.

Major version bump

The library upgrade

LangGraph 0.0.x → 0.1 has breaking changes. Every team must migrate independently, on their own schedule, with their own bugs. Migration drags on for two quarters.

The SDK absorbs the migration once. Agent code uses BaseAgent and a few stable hooks. The upgrade ships behind the SDK in days, not months.

Penetration test

The forgotten verify

One team has verify=False on an httpx client to work around a corporate proxy in dev. It ships to prod. Penetration test catches it. Severity 1.

HTTP clients are constructed by the SDK with strict defaults. Local-dev shortcuts are opt-in behind explicit settings, not default behaviour, and are flagged in audit.

Onboarding day

The tester's lament

A new engineer wants to write a unit test for an agent. Doing so requires Postgres, OPA, an LLM key, an OTel collector, and three hours of yak-shaving. Tests are skipped.

DI fakes for every dependency. container.override(FakePolicyEvaluator()). A unit test runs in 0.04 s with no external services. Tests get written.

Steady state

The drift

Eight teams' agents share five different OPA decision paths, three different log schemas, two different retry libraries, and a folklore about which env vars matter. Onboarding takes weeks. Tribal knowledge wins.

One SDK. One settings model. One observability shape. One audit shape. New engineers ship a working agent on day one, and it inherits production-grade behaviour by default.

§ 05For the business AI team

What changes for you, in plain terms.

Adopting the SDK is not a tax. It is a transfer of work. The platform team takes on the parts that have nothing to do with your business problem; you take on more of the parts that do.

Stops & starts

You stop doing

  • Writing JWT verification by hand
  • Wiring OpenTelemetry spans manually
  • Tracking tokens across LLM calls yourself
  • Maintaining your own MCP connection pool
  • Hand-rolling retry and timeout policies
  • Standing up Postgres + OPA + an LLM mock just to run a unit test
  • Designing your own audit-log schema
  • Writing your own secret resolver

You start doing more of

  • Prompt engineering and prompt evaluation
  • Domain modelling — the schemas your tools actually take and return
  • Tool design — what should be a tool, and at what granularity
  • Measuring agents against business outcomes, not infra metrics
  • Picking and tuning models for your use case
  • Iterating on your specific RAG pipeline and retrieval quality
  • Writing tests that actually catch business-logic regressions

Day one, side by side

support_agent.py — with SDK~30 lines 
from ai_core.tools import tool
from ai_core.agents import BaseAgent
from pydantic import BaseModel

class LookupTicketIn(BaseModel):
    ticket_id: str

class LookupTicketOut(BaseModel):
    status: str
    summary: str

@tool(name="lookup_ticket", version=1,
       opa_path="eaap/support/lookup")
async def lookup_ticket(p: LookupTicketIn)\
        -> LookupTicketOut:
    # your business logic, full stop.
    return await tickets.fetch(p.ticket_id)

class SupportAgent(BaseAgent):
    name = "customer-support"
    tools = [lookup_ticket]

The SDK doesn't remove work; it relocates it to the team that has signed up to do it well. You get to spend your time where it matters — on the parts of the agent that only you can build.

§ 06Common concerns

Honest answers to the questions we already heard.

It's another thing to learn.

Yes, but it replaces learning four things — OPA, OTel, LiteLLM, and LangGraph — separately. The SDK is a curated surface over those, not an addition to them. Net cognitive load: lower.

I'll lose flexibility for my edge case.

Every concern in the SDK is bound through DI. If your agent needs a different policy evaluator, observability provider, or budget service, override the binding in your module — the rest of the SDK keeps working. You replace the part you need to, not the whole stack.

My use case isn't covered.

If a feature is genuinely missing, the platform team's job is to add it once, with tests, behind a stable interface — not to leave you to fork. Bring the gap to the platform team rather than working around it in your agent.

I just want to ship a prototype, not adopt a platform.

Local-dev defaults assume you do. NoOpPolicyEvaluator, EnvSecretManager, in-memory checkpoints, and stub observability are bound by default in the dev module. You opt into production-grade bindings when you're ready, not before.

The SDK will slow my team down.

For the first agent: roughly the same speed as a careful from-scratch build. For the second, third, and fourth agents: dramatically faster, because none of the cross-cutting infrastructure has to be rebuilt. The compounding goes the right way.

§ 09The bottom line

The SDK is the contract between two teams who otherwise wouldn't agree.

The platform team gets a single place to enforce safety, security, and observability without having to police every agent code review. The business AI team gets to spend their time on the only work that actually moves the business — prompts, tools, evaluation, and domain understanding — without rebuilding infrastructure first.

The alternative is not "more flexibility for everyone." The alternative is eight slightly different implementations of the same primitives, with eight slightly different bugs, and a long calendar of incidents waiting for them to surface. We've seen that movie. We don't want to ship it.

The SDK does not slow agent teams down. Operating production AI without one is what slows everyone down — eventually, painfully, and at the worst possible moment.
§ 10Adoption path

A short, low-ceremony rollout.

  1. Week 1 · Pilot agent Pick one in-flight agent. Move it onto BaseAgent and @tool. Capture friction in a shared doc — that's the first sprint of platform-team work.
  2. Week 2–3 · Close gaps Platform team addresses the friction list. Business AI team writes the second agent on the SDK; this one should be noticeably faster.
  3. Week 4 · Production gates Set the bar: any agent shipping to a regulated environment must run on the SDK. Audit + observability shape becomes the deployment gate.
  4. Quarter 2 · Migrate the rest Existing agents migrate one by one as they touch them. No stop-the-world rewrite. The SDK absorbs library upgrades centrally; teams ride along.
  5. Steady state New agents start on the SDK from day one. New cross-cutting concerns become SDK features, not per-team workarounds. The platform improves where it should — at the platform layer.

Build it once.
Build it well.
Build it together.

What we're asking for.

/ The platform team commits to maintaining the SDK as a first-class product — with versioning, a changelog, and a clear support model.

/ The business AI team commits to building new agents on the SDK rather than around it, and to bringing gaps back to the platform team rather than forking.

/ Leadership commits to recognising the platform layer as load-bearing engineering work, not invisible plumbing.
