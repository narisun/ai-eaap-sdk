§ 01 · THE PREMISE

AI agents are easy to demo.
Operating them is the hard part.

What looks like a prototype problem…

A working demo is one prompt and a few lines of LangGraph. A working fleet is the moment every cross-cutting concern shows up at once.

Authentication. Authorisation. Audit. Observability. Retries. Timeouts. Schema versioning. Secret resolution. Token budgets. PII redaction. MCP integration. A test seam that doesn't require a live database.

…is actually a platform problem.

We can solve those concerns once, in a shared SDK we audit carefully — or we can solve them eight times, in eight teams, with eight different bugs.

This deck argues for once. It defines the two teams who'll meet at that boundary, what each one owns, and what we ask of leadership to make the model work.

§ 02 · OUR APPROACH 03 / 15
§ 02 · OUR APPROACH

One SDK. Two teams.
Zero drift.

Three principles guide every binding choice, every interface, and every line of agent code we ship.

PRINCIPLE 01

Solve cross-cutting concerns once.

Auth, audit, observability, budgets, retries, schemas, secrets — all owned in the SDK. Shared, audited, tested centrally.

PRINCIPLE 02

Depend on interfaces, bind implementations.

Every load-bearing dependency sits behind an I* interface. Implementations are swappable; the contract is stable.

PRINCIPLE 03

Absorb library churn at the SDK boundary.

When LangGraph, LiteLLM, or Pydantic move, the SDK migrates internally — once. Agents pin only the SDK.

§ 03 · THE TWO TEAMS

Two teams meet at the SDK boundary.

Adopting the SDK requires a clear split of responsibility. Each team does what they do best; neither has to do the other's work.

GROUP A · MAINTAINS THE SDK

Platform / Tech AI Team

ArchitectsSoftware EngineersDevOpsProduction Support

Owns the runtime. Implements cross-cutting concerns once, with strong tests, clear contracts, and structured error semantics. Provides safe defaults so a careless agent can't ship an unsafe configuration.

GROUP B · BUILDS ON THE SDK

Business AI Team

Agent EngineersMCP Tool AuthorsRAG EngineersDomain Experts

Owns the agents. Translates business problems into prompts, tools, schemas, and evaluations. Picks models, tunes them, measures them against business outcomes. Treats the SDK as a stable substrate.

§ 04 · PLATFORM TEAM CHARTER 05 / 15
§ 04 · WHAT THE PLATFORM TEAM OWNS

Cross-cutting concerns,
solved once for everyone.

Authentication & Authorisation
JWT verification + OPA policy at every tool and HTTP boundary; fail-closed by default.
Audit pipeline
Structured AuditRecord events; redactor chain; fan-out to file, Sentry, Datadog.
Observability
OpenTelemetry traces + LangFuse generations + structlog with task-isolated context.
Budget enforcement
Per-tenant and per-(tenant, agent) limits on tokens and USD, with daily reset.
Retry & timeout
Tenacity-backed policies with consistent jitter, status-aware backoff, cancellation.
Schema versioning
Versioned Pydantic schemas at every tool boundary; ingress + egress validation.
Secret management
Opaque references resolved through ISecretManager — env, AWS, Vault, never inline.
MCP integration
Connection pool with idle TTL and health; late-binding-safe tool resolution.
Memory & compaction
Token-aware summarisation with timeout guard; preserves Essential Entities.
DI & test seam
A real DI container is the only seam; fakes provided for every interface.

§ 05 · WHAT THE BUSINESS AI TEAM OWNS

The work only you can do.

When the SDK takes the infrastructure off your plate, this is what you spend your time on instead — the parts agentic AI actually exists to deliver.

Prompt engineering & evaluation
System prompts, few-shot examples, eval harnesses against business outcomes.
Domain modelling
The Pydantic schemas your tools take in and return — the language of the business.
Tool design
What should be a tool, at what granularity, with what policy boundary.
Model selection & tuning
Which LLM for which agent; temperature, max-tokens, fine-tuning where it pays off.
RAG pipelines
Retrieval quality, chunking, re-ranking — the parts unique to your domain knowledge.
Business-outcome metrics
Resolution rate, escalation rate, cost-per-task — not just latency & tokens.
Agent topology
Single agent vs hand-off vs supervisor; when to add an MCP server vs a tool.
Tests against fakes
Tests that catch business-logic regressions, run in 0.04s, no external services.

§ 06 · WITHOUT THE SDK 07 / 15
§ 06 · WHAT WE AVOID

What happens without a shared SDK.

02:14 · TUESDAY

The 2 a.m. incident

Billing-agent latency p99 spikes to 8s. Logs in three formats from three teams. No shared trace_id. Mean time to root-cause: hours.

$34,000 · OVERNIGHT

The midnight burn

A tool retries on every 500. By morning the agent has burned tens of thousands in inference credits. Your CFO has questions. No central budget enforcement.

Q3 · AUDIT WINDOW

The compliance audit

Regulator asks for a complete trail of AI-driven decisions. Logs are scattered across four sources with four schemas. Some events were never written.

MAJOR VERSION BUMP

The library upgrade

LangGraph ships breaking changes. Every team migrates independently, on their own schedule, with their own bugs. The migration drags on for two quarters.

§ 10 · EVOLUTION, NOT LOCK-IN 11 / 15
§ 10 · EVOLUTION OVER TIME

The SDK is the dampening layer.

LITELLM 1.32 → 1.40
Streaming response shape changed. Diff lived in LiteLLMClient._normalize_response. Agent code: zero changes.
LANGGRAPH 0.0.50 → 0.0.55
Reducer signature broadened with REMOVE_ALL_MESSAGES. SDK adopted internally; compaction node updated. Agent code: zero changes.
FUTURE · LANGGRAPH 1.0
Anticipated graph-API stabilisation. SDK absorbs the migration once. Agents using BaseAgent + @tool: expected zero changes.
FUTURE · PYDANTIC v3
SDK pins to v2 until v3 is stable, then migrates schemas centrally with explicit CHANGELOG entries. Idiomatic agent code: minimal touch-up.
The promise: The SDK pins major versions; agents pin only the SDK. Breaking changes in dependencies are absorbed inside the SDK. Deprecations get a release-cycle of warning before removal.

§ 11 · ADOPTION PATH 12 / 15
§ 11 · ADOPTION PATH

A short, low-ceremony rollout.

No stop-the-world rewrite. No big-bang. Existing agents migrate as they're touched; new agents start on the SDK from day one.

PHASE 01
Week 1 · Pilot

Pick one in-flight agent. Move it onto BaseAgent + @tool. Capture friction in a shared doc.

PHASE 02
Week 2 – 3 · Close gaps

Platform team addresses the friction list. Business team writes the second agent — noticeably faster.

PHASE 03
Week 4 · Production gates

Set the bar: any agent shipping to a regulated environment runs on the SDK. Audit + observability shape becomes the deployment gate.

PHASE 04
Quarter 2 · Migrate the rest

Existing agents migrate one by one as they touch them. New agents start on the SDK from day one.

§ 13 · THE ASK 14 / 15
§ 13 · WHAT WE'RE ASKING FOR

Three commitments from leadership.

Recognise the SDK as a first-class product.

Not invisible plumbing. Versioning, a CHANGELOG, a clear support model, and time on the platform team's roadmap. Without this, the SDK becomes someone's side project and the model collapses.

Mandate the SDK for production deployments after pilot validation.

Once the pilot agent ships, the deployment gate is "runs on the SDK". This prevents fragmentation before it happens — much cheaper than fixing it after.

Trust the platform/business contract.

Don't ask the platform team to ship business outcomes. Don't ask the business AI team to maintain platform infrastructure. Each team is set up for what they do best — let them.
